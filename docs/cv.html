RESEARCH EXPERIENCE

  • Tree Ring Data Sythesis and an Analysis of Low Frequency Climate Variability in Tree Ring Chronologies 2018-Current
    Toby Ault -- Cornell University, Ithaca, NY

    Raw tree-ring data is synthesized into a comprehensive paleoclimate database, this data is detrended using a suite of detrending models to construct tree-ring chronologies using data from the International Tree Ring Database, and the resulting chronologies are analyzed using multi-taper spectrum estimation and singular-value decomposition for signal detection and signal reconstruction.


  • Effect of Volcanic Eruptions on Hurricane Statistics in the Last Millennium 2016-2018
    Toby Ault -- Cornell University, Ithaca, NY

    Thermodynamic and dynamic effects of volcanic eruptions on hurricane statistics were examined using long forced and un-forced simulations from the Community Earth System Model. Potential and simulated hurricane statistics were computed for both simulations using potential intensity and dynamical-downscalling, respectively. Results suggest only small effects in averages of intensity, frequency, and lifetime over all last millennium eruptions.


  • Immersive Telepresence with an Aerial Vehicle 2015-2016
    Steve Marschner -- Cornell University, Ithaca, NY

    A research proposal was written for developing a systems solution for immersive real-time viewing while flying miniature aerial vehicles. A miniature aerial vehicle was built from scratch which could support a sensor array to stream environmental data to a remote workstation for real-time 3D reconstruction. Preliminary work was performed on algorithmic solutions to overcoming bandwidth and latency restrictions for this real-time reconstruction. This proposal served as my Masters thesis.


  • Aerodynamic Sound from Head-related Turbulence 2014-2015
    Doug James -- Cornell University, Ithaca, NY

    The goal of this work was to construct a model for sound resulting from wind flow around a human head. Extensive fluid dynamics simulation using human head geometry was performed to generate sound textures associated with varying incoming-wind velocity vectors. Initial attempts at synthesizing sound for arbitrary wind directions from these textures were performed using auto-regressive time stepping. This method is to be used within immersive virtual experiences such as sky-diving demos on a virtual reality headset.


  • Aerodynamics of Falling Maple Seeds 2013-2014
    Jane Wang -- Cornell University, Ithaca, NY

    For this project initial attempts were made to numerically simulate motion of falling airfoils geometrically similar to maple seeds. Experiments were also conducted on falling airfoils of varying geometry, videos of which were to enable empirical determination of aerodynamic forces and torques on the airfoils. Preliminary image processing on these videos was performed in an attempt to extract force and torque expressions to be used in further numerical simulation.


  • Expansion of Ring Bose-Einstein Condensates 2011-2013
    Mark Edwards -- Georgia Southern University, Statesboro, GA

    The focus of this project was analytical modelling of bose-einstein condensate expansion after being released from a ring trap. A langragian variational approach was used to construct an approximate analytical solution to the Gross-Pitaevski equation for steady-state within the ring trap and after release. This agreed well with numerical simulations also conducted during the project. Results were published in Physical Review E.


  • Prototyping Bragg-type Atom Interferometers 2010-2011
    Mark Edwards and Charles Clark -- Georgia Southern University, Statesboro, GA and NIST, Gaithersburg, MD

    This project also relied on a lagrangian variational approach to construct an approximate analytical solution for bose-einstein condensate behavior after being subjected to differently timed bragg pulses. Interference patterns resulting from single splitting and re-combining of bose-einstein condensates through application of bragg pulses were predicted using our analytical model. Experiments were conducted at NIST using the same sequence of bragg pulses and confirmed our predictions. This work served as my undergraduate thesis, and was published in Physical Review A.


  • Engineering Bose-Einsten Condensate Momentum Distributions 2009-2010
    Mark Edwards -- Georgia Southern University, Statesboro, GA

    Sequences of standing-wave laser pulses are able to split a stationary bose-einstein condensate into a combination of multiple momentum states. In this project an analytical treatment was used to describe a condensate after N pulses with varying time-intervals. Numerical least-squares optimization was performed using this description to determine time intervals and laser intensities required to engineer specified momentum distributions. This work was published in Physical Review A.
